Module 1

Budgeting

A budget is a plan for your money. It tells your money where to go instead of wondering where it went. The 50/30/20 rule is one of the simplest frameworks to get started.

🎯 By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  • Apply the 50/30/20 rule to divide any income into needs, wants, and savings
  • Distinguish between needs and wants using real-world examples
  • Build a personal monthly budget using the interactive calculator

The 50/30/20 Rule

50% Needs: essentials like food, transport, your phone plan.
30% Wants: entertainment, dining out, clothing, hobbies.
20% Savings: money you set aside to grow and protect your future.

The 50/30/20 split: a simple starting point for any income level.

Irregular income? Not everyone earns a steady paycheck. If your income changes month to month (from chores, gig work, or seasonal jobs), try a percentage-based approach instead of fixed amounts. Saving 20% of whatever you earn that month still builds the habit, even if the number varies.

Why it matters

Needs are non-negotiables: food, transport to school or work, essential utilities. Without them, your daily life is seriously impacted.

Wants are extras that make life enjoyable but are optional. There's nothing wrong with wants. The key is funding needs and savings first, then enjoying what's left.

Budgeting isn't about deprivation. It's about being intentional, so that when you do spend on wants, you're choosing to, not just drifting.

Module 2

Compound Interest

When your savings earn interest, that interest earns its own interest. This creates a snowball effect that grows faster the longer it runs.

🎯 By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  • Explain how compound interest differs from simple interest in your own words
  • Describe why starting to save earlier produces dramatically more growth
  • Use the calculator to estimate real savings growth with your own numbers

The simple idea: You earn interest on your money. Then you earn interest on that interest. Then on that. Over decades, this becomes extraordinary.

The formula: A = P × (1 + r/n)n×t + future value of monthly contributions. You don't need to memorise this — the calculator below handles the maths. The key idea: time is your most valuable input.

Savings snowball: each year, interest is earned on both your deposits and all previously earned interest.

Why starting early matters so much

If Emma saves $100/month from age 16 at 6% annual return, she'll have around $200,000 by age 65. If Jake starts the same habit at age 26 (just 10 years later ) he'll have roughly $100,000. Same effort, half the outcome.

The difference is the time compound interest had to work. Those extra 10 years give interest time to compound on itself exponentially.

It works the other way too. Credit card debt at 20% interest grows the same way against you. Understanding this dynamic is just as important as knowing how to grow savings.

Module 3

Savings Goals

A savings goal turns a wish into a plan. Set a specific target, a deadline, and a monthly amount, giving your money a clear purpose.

🎯 By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  • Write a SMART savings goal with a specific target and deadline
  • Calculate how much to save per month to reach any goal on time
  • Identify at least two habits that support consistent saving
Specific · Measurable · Achievable · Relevant · Time-bound

Example: "Save $800 for a laptop in 8 months" is a SMART goal. "Save more money" is not.

Tips that actually work
💸

Pay yourself first

When you get paid, move your savings amount immediately, before spending anything else. Remove the temptation to spend first.

🛡️

Build an emergency cushion

Before saving for fun goals, aim for a small cushion of $200–$500 to protect yourself from unexpected costs without going into debt.

⏱️

Use the 24-hour rule

Before any impulse purchase over $20, wait 24 hours. You'll often find you don't actually want it and that money stays in your goal fund.

📝

Track spending for one month

Log every purchase for 30 days. Most people are surprised how much small, frequent purchases (coffees, snacks, subscriptions). They all add up.

Short-term vs. Long-term goals

Not all goals are equal:
- Short-term goals (weeks to months) keep you motivated and build the saving habit.
- Long-term goals (years) are where compound interest and consistent effort really pay off.

Examples of short-term goals: a new game ($60), a gift for someone ($40), a pair of headphones ($120).
Examples of long-term goals: a car ($5,000+), a gap year travel fund ($3,000), an emergency fund ($1,000).

Having both types keeps you motivated in the short run while building towards something meaningful in the long run.

Module 4

Modern Financial Tools

From high-interest savings accounts to crypto. Here's a practical, balanced overview of the financial tools you're hearing about, and what's worth your attention right now.

🎯 By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  • Compare financial tools by risk level and suitability for beginners
  • Explain why building a financial foundation comes before higher-risk tools
  • Identify at least one low-risk tool appropriate for your current situation
Before making any financial decisions involving real money, talk to a trusted adult or a licensed financial advisor.

Tools by risk level
Low Risk
🏦

High-Interest Savings Accounts (HISA)

A regular savings account that pays a higher interest rate than standard accounts (often 3–5)%. Your money is safe and insured. A great home for your emergency fund or short-term goals. Best for: beginners.

Low Risk
📱

Savings & Budgeting Apps

Apps like Mint, YNAB, or your bank's own app help you track spending and set goals automatically. Many are free. Best for: building habits without much effort.

Medium Risk
📊

Index Funds & ETFs

Investing apps like Wealthsimple or Fidelity let you buy small pieces of hundreds of companies at once. Value goes up and down, but historically grows over long periods. Best for: long-term goals (5+ years away).

High Risk
₿

Cryptocurrency

Digital currencies like Bitcoin or Ethereum can rise dramatically and fall just as fast. Highly speculative, and not regulated like bank accounts. Don't invest money you can't afford to lose.

High Risk
📉

Individual Stock Picking

Buying shares in a single company. Even professionals rarely beat the market consistently. High potential reward, but high potential loss. More appropriate once you have a solid financial foundation.

The golden rule

Before chasing returns on any investment, make sure your financial foundation is solid: you have a budget, an emergency fund, and you're saving consistently. Higher-risk tools only make sense on top of that base, not instead of it.

If something sounds too good to be true (guaranteed 100% returns, no risk), it almost certainly is. Skepticism is a financial superpower.
